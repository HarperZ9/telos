## Local Steelman

The strongest objections are:

1. The no-cloning theorem is too standard to prove product value by itself. It proves packet mechanics, not quantum-market urgency.
2. The CNOT probe is intentionally tiny. It catches the basis-versus-superposition trap, but does not validate a real quantum workflow, noise model, compiler, cloud backend, or hardware result.
3. The market map is source-backed positioning, but the proof-receipt gaps are inferred. A buyer could argue that notebooks, cloud job metadata, SDK result objects, and hardware calibration pages already provide enough evidence for early-stage work.
4. The packet has no resource-estimation layer. For practical quantum claims, buyers need qubit counts, gate counts, depth, error assumptions, logical/physical qubit estimates, and cost/runtime estimates.
5. The packet has no backend metadata schema. Without backend, noise, calibration, queue, transpilation, and seed receipts, it cannot distinguish simulator success from hardware-relevant evidence.
